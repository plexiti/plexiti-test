Words and Phrases for Remote System Communication

I recently read a lot about “asynchronous”, “events”, “push”, “pub/sub” and similar stuff. :-) Therefore I decided to sort out the terms a bit.

I regularly read phrases as “synchronous request/reply like REST” or e.g. “pub/sub patterns requiring messaging”. As my brain always reaches out to “understand” a more complete truth hidden beneath statements :-) it then subconsciously starts to work on the concepts and terms. In this case the typical lingo used when people talk about system-to-system communication.

Tolerant readers complain seldomly :-)

While my brain goes into digesting mode, somebody might already shout: “Wrong!” :-) I often prefer to stay quiet. Most of the time such statements are really “close enough” anyway. We understand each other in the specific context of what is being said. People already know that REST can be used in a more “asynchronous” fashion of communication, too. And yes: one won’t necessarily need messaging infrastructure for a publish/subscribe paradigm of delivering some infos. In a given context, though, it might well be the easiest solution to realise the paradigm.

Digesting the system communication lingo

But then, I felt it is useful for myself to write down some of the more prominent lingo like asynchronous, events and pub/sub and ask myself: what are the real “categories of the same thing”? What are therefore things that exclude each other? Because: if two things do not exclude each other, they can quite often be combined with each other! Being aware of that results in more conscious and therefore more useful knowledge to me.

A small disclaimer: I am talking here in the context of quite “high level”, business domain related communication in between remote and distributed systems like e.g. Microservices. When going down to the lower, more technical layers of systems communication, concepts and terms might be used with (more or less) different meanings.

Recipients. Which partner(s) do we have?

A Point-to-Point model of communication transmits a message from one single sender to one single recipient. The recipient is offering a means of receiving a message, the sender decides to make use of it.

A Publish-and-Subscribe model of communication transmits a message from one sender to (potentially) many recipients. The sender acts as “publisher” by offering a message to a more general public, the potential recipients become “subscribers” when they decide to make use of it.

Acknowledgement. Do we care about delivery?

Positive Acknowledgement based communication means that successful delivery is explicitly acknowledged by the recipient.

Negative Acknowledgement based communication means that just an unsuccessful delivery is explicitly rejected by the recipient.

Reply. Do we talk one-way or two-ways?

Fire/Forget refers to a one-way communications pattern. The sender does not care about a specific business domain related answer or reaction. However, it might still care about an acknowledgement.

Request/Reply refers to a two-way communications pattern. The sender needs a specific business domain related reply or reaction. It might also care about an acknowledgement.

Execution. Do we block until completion?

Synchronous communication blocks the sending process until the business domain related operation on the recipient’s side completes.

Asynchronous communication does not block the sending process until the business domain related operation on the recipient’s side completes. The sender either does not care about any such operation or does care, but only initiates it.

Intent. What is the purpose of communication?

An Event communicates a relevant fact of the business domain. The sender informs that something relevant occurred on its side.

A Command communicates that a business domain relevant operation should happen on the recipient’s side.

A Document (message) communicates the current status of the business domain on the sender’s side.

Direction. Who is the initiating partner?

Push direction means that the sender initiates the transmission of business domain related information to the receiver. It is “event driven” in the sense that the sender identifies information potentially interesting for the receiver and transmits it.

Pull direction means that the receiver initiates the transmission of business domain related information to itself. It is “demand driven” in the sense that the sender passively waits until a recipient interrupts by requesting content.

Architecture. Which style do we use?

REST (Representational State Transfer) enables separate applications to work together by requesting and manipulating textual representations of resources using a uniform and predefined set of stateless operations. It relies on a cacheable client-server communications protocol – and in virtually all cases, the HTTP protocol is used.

Messaging enables separate applications to work together in a decoupled fashion such that applications can be easily added or removed without affecting the others. A connecting message-oriented middleware is used that allows different systems to exchange messages through a shared set of interfaces. It relies on a client-broker protocol such as AMQP.
