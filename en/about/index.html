About the business programmer blog

A colorful animal walks on a flower meadow and meets a frog. The frog asks what kind of animal it is. But the small colorful animal does not know a good answer. So now it asks other animals (horses, fish, birds, hippos, parrots, dogs) if anyone knew who it was. But no one knows it. The little animal - increasingly desperate - finally asks: “What if I do not exist at all?” But then - all of a sudden - it is hit upon an idea. And the small colorful animal knows: “Certainly - I exist: - I am me!“

Even as a child I was a fan of the “Little I-am-me”! The well-known children’s classic by Mira Lobe and Susi Weigel tells of a happy ending search for identity. And there I see striking parallels. Because if I am asked what I do for a living, I do often not have a good answer! I am neither a classic “business analyst” nor a classic “software developer”! And on bad days I ask myself whether I exist at all!

I am deeply into many programming topics, usually much deeper than it is the case with most “business analysts”. On the other hand , I am somewhat less interested in the “purely technical” topics as it should be the case with “software developers”! Uptimes and reliable data exchange, scalability or performance optimisation is in my view better taken care of by those people who are fascinated by these subjects.

I am fascinated by those things that other software developers are sometimes less interested in than it could be the case. The business logic appeals to me, the inner purpose of software, even the economic justification for its existence. But then all the little functional (!) details, which when they happen to “really work” make software so valuable to the “business”. I get enthusiastic about automated test specifications as well as about modeling directly executable processes or preparing visualisations of statistical data for management. I work at my best, if I can talk with subject matter experts about their ideas directly and then not only describe what they need, but program it. As part of a development team, in the midst of those people who get excited about reliably reachable services, scalable systems and performing algorithms. And if I get along well with those people, I sometimes even write quite understandable documentation for them . (On my way, I will program the adjustments I need for the documentation site generator!)

I am me, quite definitely. But I also know that the world needs terms and concepts, in order to communicate well with each other. That’s why I call myself today simply a business programmer. What’s that all about? Oh well. Of course, all the stuff I am going to write about here!
